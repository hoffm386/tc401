Relationships & Facebook

  • Pro

    Weak Ties and Social Capital

    Relationships include weak ties as well as the more-obvious strong ties. Facebook allows you to maintain beneficial weak ties with more people than would be possible without it. While on the surface this means that users have an easier time finding less-close friends who might want to hang out at the beach, it also has concrete benefits. A study performed by Facebook Data Science showed that these weak ties have positive effects on users' ability to find employment and objective information from friendly sources. The simple fact that the median Facebook user has, on average, 200 friends also means that Facebook promotes social capital, a commodity promoted by the Harvard political scientist Robert D. Putnam in his book Bowling Alone: The Collapse and Revival of American Community.

  • Con

    Loss of Non-Verbal Cues

    While the connections to weak ties benefit the individuals involved, they aren't necessarily strong relationships. Facebook, like any other Computer-Mediated Communication (CMC), has limitations on what content can be transmitted over it. Even though the Social Information Processing Theory argues that people will "translate" nonverbal cues into text, the fact remains that some researchers have found that 93% of communication is nonverbal. Any long-distance relationships maintained purely via Facebook will likely be hampered by this dramatic reduction in the amount of information they can convery with a sentence.

  • Pro

    Facebook Can Forge Closer Connections

    According to Tidwell and Walther, a communication medium like the instant messaging available on Facebook leads people to disclose more personal information than they would in person. According to the Social Penetration Theory, relationships grow closer as the individuals share more personal information with each other. This may be related to the fact that Facebook provides a medium for uncertainty reduction and allows people to predict others' behavior and respond appropriately to it. Therefore Facebook encourages closer relationships because it promotes more delf-disclosure and reduces the need for conversations about "the small stuff".

  • Con

    Some of Those Connections Could be Lying or Misinterpreted

    Even though people on Facebook might seem to be disclosing more personal information, users have a hard time knowing whether they are being deceived. Unlike traditional in-person relationships, Facebook interactions can involve identity-based deceptions without any extreme effort on the part of the deceiver -- all they have to do is put up a photo of another person. The potential for message-based deception is there, too, since two of the three categories of leakage cues (indicators that someone is lying) are Biometric/Physical and Body Movement, and neither of them are visible over Facebook. Also, even if intentional deception isn't happening, online interactions can still lead to misconceptions. The Hyper-Personal Model, built on Social Information Processing Theory, shows how idealized versions of people can be transmitted online, meaning that the online connection is superficial and an in-person meeting is likely to lead to disappointment.
